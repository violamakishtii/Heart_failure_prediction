Based on the results from the four models trained on the heart disease dataset, Naive Bayes performed best with 91.3% accuracy, suggesting that even under the assumption of feature independence, the dataset's features are strong and clean enough predictors of heart disease. Logistic Regression followed closely at 88.6%, confirming that the relationship between the features and the target is largely linear, meaning factors like ST_Slope, ChestPainType, and Oldpeak have a fairly direct and measurable influence on whether a patient has heart disease. Linear Regression, despite not being designed for classification, achieved 89.1% when its output was thresholded at 0.5, which is surprisingly competitive but unreliable in practice since it can predict values outside the 0–1 range. The Decision Tree, while the most visually interpretable model with its plotted branches and splits, scored the lowest at 83.7%, likely because limiting max_depth=4 prevents it from capturing more complex patterns, though this also protects it from overfitting. Overall, the dataset of 918 patients with 12 clinical features such as age, cholesterol, resting blood pressure, and ECG results provides enough signal for all four models to perform well above chance, and the high recall scores across models (especially Naive Bayes at 93.3%) indicate that the models are particularly good at correctly identifying patients who do have heart disease  which is the most clinically important outcome.
